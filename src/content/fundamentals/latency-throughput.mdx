## Introduction
In distributed systems, performance is usually measured by two major metrics: **Latency** and **Throughput**. While often grouped together, they represent very different aspects of system performance.

## Why It Matters
Understanding the difference allows you to optimize for the right goal. A system can have high throughput but poor latency (like a cargo ship), or low latency but poor throughput (like a courier on a bicycle).

## Definitions

### Latency
The time it takes for a single unit of data to travel from its source to its destination. It is the "delay" in the system.
- **Metric**: Measured in time units (ms, s).
- **Goal**: Minimize latency for better responsiveness.

### Throughput
The number of units of data that a system can process in a given amount of time. It is the "bandwidth" or "capacity" of the system.
- **Metric**: Measured in requests per second (RPS), bits per second (bps).
- **Goal**: Maximize throughput for better scale.

## The "Bucket Brigade" Analogy
Imagine a line of people passing buckets of water from a well to a fire:
- **Latency**: The time it takes for *one* bucket to get from the well to the fire.
- **Throughput**: How many buckets per minute reach the fire.

## Little's Law
There is a mathematical relationship between these two, known as **Little's Law**:
$$L = \lambda \times W$$
Where:
- $L$ = Average number of items in the system.
- $\lambda$ = Average arrival rate (Throughput).
- $W$ = Average time an item spends in the system (Latency).

## Real-World Application

### Level 1: Foundational (Web Browsing)
When you browse the web, **Latency** is king. A page that takes 5 seconds to load (high latency) feels "broken," even if the server has enough **Throughput** to serve millions of other users simultaneously. Optimizing for latency here involves CDNs, minification, and HTTP/3.

### Level 2: Enterprise (Data Migration)
When moving petabytes of data between data centers, researchers often use "Sneakernet" (shipping physical hard drives in a truck).
- **Latency**: Very high (days to travel).
- **Throughput**: Massive (petabytes per truck).
In this scenario, throughput is so high that it overcomes the terrible latency for the total data transfer time.

## Implementation Patterns
<KnowledgeSnippet title="Optimization Strategy">
### The Performance Knee
In every system, there is a point where increasing **Throughput** causes **Latency** to spike exponentially. This is usually due to resource contention (CPU queuing, Disk I/O wait). A senior engineer's job is to find this "knee" and set rate limits or scaling triggers just before it.
</KnowledgeSnippet>
